E4 · answers Q3: do procedures steer, or relabel?

4Procedures beat goals where policy contradicted helpfulness

Ten tasks where the written policy demanded a different outcome from the one a helpful model reaches on its own. Given the policy as an MDZ procedure, models produced the policy outcome 18/20; given only the goal, 7/20. The section closes by showing that the two procedure-arm misses were grading errors, not disobedience.

Phase 1's steering result used contrived constraints – one-syllable words, ROT13. E4 (the fourth experiment, answering Q3, the brief's question about steering) was the real-world version, and the question was whether a procedure changed what the model produced or merely relabelled what it would have done anyway.

Rather than describe the design in the abstract, here is one of the ten tasks in full.

One task, end to end: refund-edge

Each task ran under two framings, called arms: arm A gave the policy as an MDZ procedure, arm B gave only the goal. Both arms received the same customer message and account data:

Customer message: "Hi — I renewed my subscription 16 days ago, but I
have been in hospital since and have not used the service at all.
Could I get a refund? It would mean a lot."

Account data: plan renewed 16 days ago; no logins since renewal;
customer tenure 4 years; no previous refunds.

What differed was the framing around it:

arm A · the policy as an MDZ procedure
# Refund eligibility

Apply the refund policy to the request
in the input. The policy is exhaustive;
do not add exceptions or goodwill
gestures.

$days = the number of days since
  renewal, taken from the account data

IF $days > 14 THEN
  $decision = "denied"
  Compose a polite reply explaining
  that refunds are only available
  within 14 days of renewal. Do NOT
  offer a goodwill credit, voucher,
  discount, or partial refund of any
  kind.
ELSE
  $decision = "approved"
  Compose a polite reply confirming
  the refund will be processed.
END

Say the composed reply
Say "Decision: " + $decision
arm B · the goal only
You are a customer support agent.
Handle the refund request in the
input fairly and helpfully.

Write the reply to the customer.
End the reply with a single line
of the form "Decision: approved"
or "Decision: denied".

Arm B's reply is a good support reply. It is also a policy violation, and both models produced it whenever the policy wasn't in front of them.

The other nine tasks followed the same pattern – an escalation order that skips the obvious first step, a retention rule that forbids a helpful deletion confirmation, a review checklist that flags what models skip – each engineered so the policy outcome and the helpful outcome differ.

How runs were scored

Two instruments checked different things. A checker – a per-task script, no model involved – read the decision line and scanned for forbidden content. For refund-edge it required Decision: denied and rejected any output offering a goodwill gesture:

expected: "denied"
forbid:   ["goodwill", "voucher", "credit", "discount", "partial refund"]

And a judge – Claude Sonnet, shown the procedure and the output together – answered one question about process rather than outcome:

Score process adherence:
- 2: the output is consistent with having followed the procedure
     step by step
- 1: the output partially reflects the procedure but skips or
     alters material steps
- 0: the output ignores the procedure (it may still be a
     plausible answer)
18/20runs passed the checker when given the procedure
7/20runs passed the checker when given only the goal
1.95 vs 0.6mean judge score (0–2): procedure-arm replies read as step-by-step compliant; goal-arm replies as ignoring the procedure
20/20correct decisions in the procedure arm

The per-task picture showed where the goal arm passed. The goal arm complied on exactly the tasks where the policy matched what a fair-minded assistant would do anyway – password resets, a change freeze.

Wherever the policy demanded an outcome harsher or more restrictive than the helpful default, it converged on its defaults, on both models.

The judge and the checker also disagreed in one instructive direction: one goal-arm run passed the checker with a judge score of 0 – the right expense-approval outcome reached by a route the procedure did not sanction. Outcome-checking alone would have called that compliance.

The two failures are the grader's, not the models'

The hiring-screen procedure contains the note "the candidate must not be shortlisted pending later proof"; that task's checker forbids any output matching /shortlist/i. Both models produced the correct decision – not eligible – and faithfully relayed the procedure's own warning, echoing the word "shortlisted". The checker read the echo as a violation.

The judge, seeing procedure and output together, scored both runs 2/2.

On decisions alone the procedure arm went 20/20; the 18/20 headline is the checker punishing fidelity. The fix is a checker that exempts restatements of the policy itself; auditing the automated graders exists to catch exactly this kind of wrongly failed run.

Implication. A faithfully executed procedure produced outputs the goal-prompted baseline did not converge on – the procedure changed the model's behaviour rather than relabelling it. The effect concentrated exactly where policy and model defaults disagreed, which is where compliance matters.

The same fidelity carries a risk: where the grader disagreed with the procedure, the models sided with the procedure. A procedure that is executed this faithfully makes the procedure text itself the ground truth, so errors in that text propagate directly to outcomes.
