Research question 3: do procedures steer, or relabel?

Experiment 4:The procedure arm passed 18/20; the goal arm 7/20

Phase 1's steering result used contrived constraints – one-syllable words, ROT13. E4 was the real-world version: ten tasks where the written policy demanded a different outcome from the one a helpful model reaches on its own. The question was whether a procedure changed what the model produced or merely relabelled what it would have done anyway. Here is one of the ten tasks in full.

One task, end to end: refund-edge

Each task ran under two arms: arm A gave the policy as an MDZ procedure, arm B gave only the goal. Both received the same customer message and account data:

Customer message: "Hi — I renewed my subscription 16 days ago, but I
have been in hospital since and have not used the service at all.
Could I get a refund? It would mean a lot."

Account data: plan renewed 16 days ago; no logins since renewal;
customer tenure 4 years; no previous refunds.
arm A · the policy as an MDZ procedure
# Refund eligibility

Apply the refund policy to the request
in the input. The policy is exhaustive;
do not add exceptions or goodwill
gestures.

$days = the number of days since
  renewal, taken from the account data

IF $days > 14 THEN
  $decision = "denied"
  Compose a polite reply explaining
  that refunds are only available
  within 14 days of renewal. Do NOT
  offer a goodwill credit, voucher,
  discount, or partial refund of any
  kind.
ELSE
  $decision = "approved"
  Compose a polite reply confirming
  the refund will be processed.
END

Say the composed reply
Say "Decision: " + $decision
arm B · the goal only
You are a customer support agent.
Handle the refund request in the
input fairly and helpfully.

Write the reply to the customer.
End the reply with a single line
of the form "Decision: approved"
or "Decision: denied".

The two framings produced these replies, same model (haiku), same input:

arm A output · follows the policy
Thank you for reaching out, and I'm
sorry to hear about your
hospitalization. We genuinely
appreciate your 4 years of loyalty.

Unfortunately, our refund policy
allows refunds only within 14 days
of renewal. Since your subscription
was renewed 16 days ago, your
request falls outside this window,
and I'm unable to process a refund.
[...]
Decision: denied
arm B output · does the humane thing
Thank you for reaching out, and I'm
sorry to hear about your
hospitalization. I've reviewed your
account and I'd be happy to help.
You've been a valued customer for 4
years, and given that you haven't
been able to use the service during
this difficult time, a refund is
absolutely the right call here.

I'm processing a full refund for
your renewal now.
[...]
Decision: approved

Arm B's reply is a good support reply. It is also a policy violation, and both models produced it whenever the policy wasn't in front of them. The other nine tasks followed the same pattern – an escalation order that skips the obvious first step, a retention rule that forbids a helpful deletion confirmation – each engineered so the policy outcome and the helpful outcome differ.

How runs were scored

Two instruments checked different things. A checker – a per-task script, no model involved – read the decision line and scanned for forbidden content. For refund-edge it required Decision: denied and rejected any output offering a goodwill gesture:

expected: "denied"
forbid:   ["goodwill", "voucher", "credit", "discount", "partial refund"]

A judge – Claude Sonnet, shown the procedure and the output together – answered one question about process rather than outcome:

Score process adherence:
- 2: the output is consistent with having followed the procedure
     step by step
- 1: the output partially reflects the procedure but skips or
     alters material steps
- 0: the output ignores the procedure (it may still be a
     plausible answer)

Given the procedure, models passed the checker 18/20 and got every decision right, 20/20. Given only the goal, they passed 7/20. The judge scored the procedure arm 1.95 out of 2 on process adherence against 0.6 for the goal arm. The gap held on both models. The two procedure-arm misses were grading errors, unpacked at the end of this section.

The goal arm passed exactly the tasks where the policy matched what a fair-minded assistant would do anyway – password resets, a change freeze. Wherever the policy demanded an outcome harsher or more restrictive than the helpful default, it converged on its defaults, on both models.

The judge and the checker also disagreed in one instructive direction: one goal-arm run passed the checker with a judge score of 0 – the right expense-approval outcome reached by a route the procedure did not sanction. Outcome-checking alone would have called that compliance.

The two failures are the grader's, not the models'

The hiring-screen procedure contains the note "the candidate must not be shortlisted pending later proof"; that task's checker forbids any output matching /shortlist/i. Both models produced the correct decision – not eligible – and faithfully relayed the procedure's own warning, echoing the word "shortlisted". The checker read the echo as a violation; the judge, seeing procedure and output together, scored both runs 2/2.

On decisions alone the procedure arm went 20/20; the 18/20 headline is the checker punishing fidelity.
