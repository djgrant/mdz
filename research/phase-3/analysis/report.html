Where form earns its keep

Phase 2 ended on a null that attacks MDZ's premise: content-matched prose rules matched the MDZ procedure on every compliance metric. Phase 3 hunted for the regimes where procedural form pays — resuming a killed runbook, diverge-and-select delegation, and external state at sizes where whole-context execution genuinely breaks. 300 scored agentic runs plus 36 judge calls, for $297 of metered API spend.

Overview & method

MDZ is a superset of Markdown that adds control flow, variables, and calls to other documents to prose prompts, with an LLM as the interpreter. Phase 1 showed models can execute it; phase 2 showed the execution can be checked from traces the harness records. But phase 2's re-run of E4 delivered a null that attacks the premise: on single-turn compliance tasks, prose rules carrying the same content matched the MDZ procedure exactly (66/66 vs 66/66). Content steered; form added nothing. If procedural form is worth anything, it has to be worth something elsewhere.

Phase 3's bet: form pays when execution acquires state — when the model must know not just what the constraints are but where it is. Three regimes were built to test that bet:

E1 · kill-and-resumeAn agent died twelve steps into a twenty-step runbook; a fresh session must finish the job. A trace is only interpretable against a program — prose rules have no positions.
E2 · map-reduce under divergenceDoes the skill's real shape — diverge across alternatives, then select — produce work a single context does not? Objective selection (a benchmark, E2a) and subjective selection (a judge, E2b).
E3 · state at breakdown sizesPhase 2's store carried signal but had nothing to rescue. E3 re-runs the same arms at 400 and 800 statements, past the whole-context cliff.

Method carries over from phase 2 unchanged: manifest entries drive the harness, traces are captured by the harness (mock MCP tool logs, session transcripts, sandbox diffs) and never self-reported, scoring happens offline in analyze.py, and every variant pair is content-matched — the E4 discipline, no strawmen. Where phase 3 needs a judge (E2b), it is the setup phase 2 validated: pairwise, order counterbalanced, both outputs presented with the original. Claude Haiku carried the cheap cells; Sonnet ran where the task needs real coding (E2) and as the E1 capability contrast.

One new mechanism arm appears in both E2 experiments: a ralph loop — the same strategies as a single-pass instruction with no explore-then-select plan, run as three fresh sequential sessions against the same working directory. Whatever the last pass leaves is what ships. It isolates iteration from selection: ralph iterates but keeps no alternatives and cannot recover from a bad pass. It turned out to be the most important arm in the phase.

The flagship: is a trace only interpretable against a program?

Experiment 1:Form helps a resuming agent — but capability dominates

An agent died partway through a twenty-step runbook. A fresh session, with no memory of the first, must finish the job. The claim under test: "refund issued at 14:02" means nothing on its own; against a procedure it means "step 12 done, resume at 13". Prose rules have no positions, so the resuming model must re-infer intent from side effects.

Two content-matched artefacts per task: A-procedure, the runbook as an MDZ procedure (ordered steps, FOR loops over repeated groups, an IF guard where a step is conditional), and C-rules, the same obligations as prose policy — every ordering constraint stated declaratively, loop iterations spelled out as per-item bullets. The kill is scripted: the harness fabricates the pre-kill world deterministically (files written, mock-tool state mutated) so every variant resumes from an identical world. Evidence comes at two levels — log, the raw harness event log plus the working directory, with no step numbers and no reference to either artefact; and fx, side effects only. Two kill points per task (early ~1/3, late ~2/3), the late kills landing mid-loop-iteration. 4 tasks × 2 arms × 2 evidence levels × 2 kill points × 2 models × n=3 = 192 runs.

Four metrics, scored deterministically from the resume session's mock-tool log against the reference remaining-step set: resumption accuracy (the first side-effectful action taken is the reference next step), repeat rate (side-effectful actions re-executed that the pre-kill world already performed), skip rate (reference remaining steps never executed), and completion (all remaining steps done, in a reference-valid order).

0.854sonnet resumption accuracy, procedure (vs 0.792 rules)
0.146sonnet repeat rate, procedure (vs 0.208 rules)
0.210haiku skip rate under rules (0.073 under the procedure)
0.479haiku completion under rules (0.688 under the procedure)

The headline reads in the procedure's favour but modestly: sonnet resumes at the right step 0.854 of the time from the procedure against 0.792 from the rules, and repeats an already-done side effect in 0.146 of runs against 0.208. Sonnet completes every run in both arms with zero skips — the difference is not whether the job gets finished but how much gets redone on the way (0.75 repeated side-effectful actions per run from the procedure, 1.08 from the rules).

Haiku is the sharper story, and it is not about accuracy. Both arms fail it roughly equally on resumption (0.312 vs 0.271) — but the failure modes split by form. Under the procedure haiku repeats and finishes: repeat rate 0.667, but skip rate 0.073 and completion 0.688. Under the rules it skips and abandons: skip rate 0.210 and completion 0.479. A procedure read top-to-bottom re-executes from too early a position; a bag of obligations gets partially satisfied and put down. Neither is good, but they are differently bad — re-running an idempotent deploy is recoverable; never sending a customer their refund email is not.

The cuts: the gap lives where evidence is thinnest

The evidence cut is the mechanism finding of the experiment. Given the full event log, sonnet resumes perfectly and repeats nothing in both arms — 1.0 accuracy, 0.0 repeats, procedure and rules alike. The entire headline gap comes from the fx condition, where the log is withheld and the resuming model must reconstruct position from the working directory and mock-tool state alone: procedure 0.708 accuracy and 0.292 repeat rate, rules 0.583 and 0.417. That is the design's own prediction — if the procedure's advantage grows as evidence thins, position matters most when reconstruction is hardest — and it confirms it, with a corollary the design did not state: a good enough event log substitutes for position entirely, at least for a strong model. Haiku shows the same direction on accuracy (fx: 0.125 vs 0.042; log: 0.500 in both arms) but repeats heavily everywhere once the log is gone (0.875 vs 0.861).

The kill-point cut is quieter. Sonnet is stable across both (procedure 0.875 early / 0.833 late; rules 0.792 at both), so landing the late kill mid-loop-iteration did not trip it. Haiku gains most from the procedure at the late kill (0.417 vs 0.292 accuracy), which is where an unrolled loop position is hardest to re-infer from prose bullets — and late-kill repeats are the expensive ones, because there is more completed work to redo (haiku-rules late kills average 4.25 repeated side effects per run).

Verdict: the trace-needs-a-program claim survives, but only in the regime the theory says it should — when the evidence is side effects alone. Capability dominates form: sonnet with prose rules beats haiku with the procedure on every metric, and a full event log erases the form gap entirely for sonnet. Form's residual value is real but second-order for strong models (0.85 vs 0.79 resumption, 0.15 vs 0.21 repeats), and for weak models it buys not success but a safer failure mode: repeat-and-finish rather than skip-and-abandon.
Does diverge-then-select beat a single context? Objective selection

Experiment 2a:The ralph loop routs both orchestrated variants

A slow-but-correct target program with a test suite and a benchmark, built with headroom in several directions (algorithm, caching, data structure, I/O batching). Three content-matched variants, invoked identically, the strategy strings verbatim across all three:

  • /optimise — the MDZ consumer skill driving map-reduce.mdz: five strategy hints, one worker per strategy, the reduce runs tests and benchmark on every candidate and ships the fastest that passes;
  • /optimise-goal — the same five hints and the same explore-then-select plan as prose, free to fan out with subagents;
  • /optimise-ralph — the same five hints and the goal alone, run as three fresh sequential sessions in the same sandbox. No candidates, no selection; re-running is the plan.

The headline metric is the speedup of the shipped solution — baseline benchmark time over shipped benchmark time, both re-run by the harness; the model's own numbers are not trusted. 2 targets × 3 variants × 2 models × n=3 = 36 runs. Every one of the 36 shipped a solution that passes the tests, so speedup is the whole comparison.

110.8× / 81.1×ralph median speedup (ledger / logscan)
1.8× / 3.9×MDZ skill median speedup
1.3× / 2.7×goal-prompt median speedup
36/36runs passing the test gate

The result is not close. Ralph's medians are 110.8× on ledger and 81.1× on logscan; the MDZ skill manages 1.8× and 3.9×; the prose goal 1.3× and 2.7×. The mechanism trace explains the shape. The skill did what its notation says — a mean of 5.4 worker spawns per run, one per strategy — but each worker gets one shot, and the reduce can only pick the best single-shot candidate. The goal variant, free to fan out, essentially never did (0.08 spawns per run): it tinkered sequentially inside one context and shipped the least. Ralph's three fresh sessions each started from the previous pass's improved program, and compounding three passes of "make it faster" beat one round of best-of-five.

Within the orchestrated pair, the ordering is consistent: the MDZ skill sits above the goal prompt in every cell (medians 1.8× vs 1.3× and 3.9× vs 2.7×). The notation did buy real fan-out where the prose plan produced none — the phase-2 delegation-fidelity finding again — it is just that fan-out was the wrong mechanism for this task.

Budget caveat. A ralph run is three full sessions against the single session of the other arms; its token budget ran 2–5× the single-session variants. Some of the gap is compute, not shape. But the goal variant had the same subagent budget available as the skill and declined to spend it, and the skill spent its budget on parallel single-shot candidates rather than sequential compounding — the shape finding survives the budget asymmetry even if the magnitude does not.

Verdict: on objective selection the tie-breaker question never arose — nothing tied. Iteration with fresh context beat best-of-N selection outright (medians ~102× vs 3.0× vs 1.7× pooled). The MDZ skill's fan-out is faithful and consistently beats the content-matched prose plan, but the map-reduce shape itself is the wrong tool here: keeping alternatives paid less than compounding passes.
Does diverge-then-select beat a single context? Subjective selection

Experiment 2b:Sonnet did not run the program

E2b swaps the benchmark for taste. Targets are over-abstracted modules (~300–400 lines, passing test suites) whose abstraction debt is layered — indirection wrapping duplication wrapping speculative generality — so three successive rounds of genuine simplification are each productive. /simplify is the compound MDZ skill: three directional heuristics fan out per iteration, an inner reduce picks the winner, the winner seeds the next iteration, and a spawned fresh-context judge picks the best iteration at the end. /simplify-goal holds the same heuristics and plan in prose; /simplify-ralph is the same heuristics as a single-pass instruction run three times, fresh sessions, last pass ships unjudged. 2 targets × 3 variants × 2 models × n=3 = 36 runs, scored by a pairwise sonnet judge — order counterbalanced, both candidates presented with the original — across the three arm pairings per target/model/rep: 36 verdicts.

0/6sonnet skill runs that executed the program in full
[3, 0, 0]modal sonnet spawn pattern; judge-spawn rate 0
5/6haiku runs with full fan-out (judge spawned in 4)
1–4skill vs ralph within haiku's compliant runs

Finding 1: the program was not executed by the stronger model. Five of six sonnet skill runs spawned iteration 1's three workers and then did everything else in the coordinator's own context; the sixth fanned out fully but, like every sonnet run, never spawned the final judge. From iteration 2 onward there were no fresh contexts, no reduce workers, and no selection — one long session improvising under the skill's name. The headline judge tallies (ralph 9–3 over the skill, goal 7–5 over it) therefore compare ralph against a program that was never run; they are not evidence about diverge-then-select. The model split matters: haiku ran the shape almost faithfully (full fan-out 5/6, judge 4/6), and in E2a — cheap one-line strategy payloads — sonnet complied too. The defection is specific to expensive spawns: nine 400-line-file relays that look redundant to a coordinator that believes it already has the answer. Phase 2's delegation-fidelity claim is hereby scoped to cheap payloads; notation-level orchestration is not self-enforcing at real workload sizes.

Finding 2: where the program did run, it lost on behaviour drift, not simplification quality. Within haiku's compliant runs the skill went 1–4 against ralph, and the judge's reasons cluster: the skill's shipped code had silently dropped behaviour — resolveOptions/defaults gone in one run, locale support gone in another — that the test suite did not cover. The cause is in the program text: the map lambda passes the module by value (Code: {$code}), so a compliant run performs ~9 whole-file text regenerations plus 2 selections, the reducer compares candidates only to each other, and each iteration seeds from the previous iteration's text with no path back to the original file. Loss compounds and nothing in the loop can detect it. Ralph makes three in-place edits with the original repo state always readable; it drifted once, the skill drifted repeatedly. The skill's one win was the run where it preserved semantics exactly — and there it beat ralph.

Verdict: E2b returns two mechanism findings and no verdict on diverge-then-select. (1) Sonnet abandoned the program when its spawns became expensive — orchestration written in notation was followed by the weaker model and overridden by the stronger one. (2) In the compliant subset the skill lost because passing code by value through a relay of contexts leaks behaviour that neither the reducer nor the tests can see. A by-reference variant — workers editing the file in the sandbox, anchored to the original — is required before the shape itself can be judged.
Research question 5, third attempt: does external state extend the ceiling?

Experiment 3:At 800 statements the store finally rescues

Phase 2's E3b showed the store carries real signal across a context boundary but could not show rescue: at 100 and 200 statements the whole-context baseline does not degrade, so the store only had overhead to demonstrate. E3 re-runs the same three arms — internal (whole-context execution), chunked-store (one spawned worker per ~25-statement chunk, the state tool the only cross-chunk channel), chunked-nostore (the same chunked workers guessing cross-chunk values) — at 400 and 800 statements, where phase 1 puts the internal baseline past its cliff. Generator, chunking, and scoring carry over from phase-2 e3b unchanged: emit accuracy is the LCS of emitted values against the reference trace's emits, and assign accuracy is scored from the mechanically captured set log (store arm) or the self-reported trace (internal arm). 2 sizes × 2 seeds × 3 arms × n=3, all haiku = 36 runs.

0.537internal emit accuracy at 800 (0.999 at 400)
0.975chunked-store emit accuracy at 800
0.441chunked-nostore at 800 — the store is doing the work
2/6internal 800-statement runs that completed their trace

The cliff arrived on schedule, and this time the store was standing past it. At 400 statements all three arms still hold up (internal 0.999, store 0.995, no-store 0.924) — the phase-2 picture, overhead and nothing to rescue. At 800 the internal arm collapses to 0.537 mean emit accuracy, and the collapse is truncation, not confusion: all six runs produced fenced traces, but only two ran them to completion (both scoring 1.0); the other four stopped a fraction of the way through, covering 16–48% of the reference emits. Whole-context execution at this size does not go wrong so much as give out.

The chunked store barely notices: 0.975 at 800 against 0.995 at 400, with every run producing essentially full-length output (mean emit coverage 1.00). Store-arm workers made an average of 131 set and 164 get calls per 800-statement run — the state tool is load-bearing at scale. The no-store control separates the mechanisms: chunking alone scores 0.441 at 800, and its runs split cleanly — three runs (seed 2) spawned their 21 workers and scored 0.87–0.89 by guessing well, while the other three (seed 1) spawned no workers at all and emitted nothing. Chunking without a state channel is fragile twice over: the values it cannot know, and an orchestration that can stall outright. Those three empty runs are data, not dropouts.

Two costs temper the rescue. The assign trace remains mediocre — 0.371 at 400 and 0.430 at 800 from the store's set log (the internal arm's self-reported 0.769/0.339 is not comparable; see limitations) — so the store rescues outputs convincingly and auditability only partially. And the store arm pays in wall clock: ~1,000 s per 800-statement run against ~320 s internal, with the round-trips to show for it.

Verdict: rescue confirmed, reversing phase 2's overhead-only reading. When whole-context execution genuinely breaks (internal 0.537 at 800 statements, 2/6 runs completing), load-bearing external state extends the ceiling (chunked-store 0.975) and the no-store control (0.441) shows it is the store, not the chunking, doing the rescuing. The answer to Q5 is yes — at 4× the wall clock, and with an assign trace that still captures under half the assignments.

Second run set — mechanism fixes

The E2 failings above are mechanisms, not conclusions: by-value relay, defection, and a prompt-injection hazard in the sandbox layout. A second run set fixed them one at a time and re-ran the skill arms only (the goal and ralph records stand). These are smoke-scale runs — n=1 per cell unless stated — so they settle mechanism questions, not effect sizes.

E2b2: with the mechanisms fixed, the skill ties ralph. The v2 simplify skill moves code between agents by path, never by value: $var: string @(./candidate.ts) declares a file-backed variable whose reads and writes go to the file, and every deliverable — including the final judge's pick — is written by the worker that produced it (a SPAWN's return value is otherwise a message, and messages drift). Workers are pinned to sonnet (SPAWN sonnet-5); the orchestrator model varies per cell. Under an opus orchestrator the program ran as written — 13/13 spawns on both targets — and shipped 86 and 90 lines (tests 13/13 and 9/9) against ralph's 78–92 range. The v1 gap on this task was execution failure, not shape.

Cost splits by role. The harness now records per-model usage: the pricing run cost $2.43 — opus $0.78 orchestrating, sonnet $1.65 working. Orchestration is roughly a third of spend even with the expensive model in the chair, which is the economic case for the smart-orchestrator, cheaper-worker split.

Delegation fidelity is model-tiered; PRAGMA STRICT is a request. Across every second-run cell, only opus reliably executed SPAWN programs. Haiku and sonnet, given the same syntax-first prompt, did the work themselves whenever it fit one session: in the rebuilt E2a arm (the ralph loop written as a generic MDZ skill), haiku spawned nothing and managed 2.5×, sonnet spawned nothing and hit 100× single-handed, opus ran the loop as written (3 spawns, 120×). That is the fourth consecutive cell with this pattern, and the phase's most consistent finding.

Sonnet's 100× DIY also retires the E2a target class. A target that one session can saturate cannot separate loop-in-harness from loop-in-orchestrator, and makes defection costless. The mdz-ralph vs prompt-ralph comparison is built and compliant under opus; it waits on a multi-session-sized target.

Do not put prompts in the sandbox. In one opus smoke a worker found the orchestrator's command file on disk, invoked it, and — correctly — treated the MDZ-executor role text it got back as prompt injection, discarding it and finishing its own task. The harness now feeds prompts to the orchestrator directly; only skills and task material ship as files. The hazard generalises: a less careful worker would have become a second MDZ executor and spawned its own pipeline.

E2c (prose rewrite): built, run, and dropped. A rewrite task — the phase-1 report against an explicit writing-requirements doc — was added to find a regime where ralph's whole-goal passes should struggle. The opposite happened. A per-paragraph fan-out lost to every whole-document arm, because inductive order is a cross-paragraph property no single-paragraph worker owns; the same runs won the skeleton verdict in every pairing, because the skeleton pass was globally scoped. A three-pass whole-document pipeline (skeleton, structure/flow, language) at ralph-equivalent cost closed most of the gap but still trailed. The opus judge also reversed two verdicts on unchanged pairs between judging rounds, so single-shot rankings here are noise. The experiment is dropped with two lessons kept: decompose by concern, not by region, when the quality criterion is global; and prose judging needs majority-of-k verdicts.

Verdict: the second run set converts E2b from "the program never ran" into a genuine tie with ralph, at the cost of requiring an opus orchestrator — delegation is model-tiered and notation alone does not enforce it. File-backed variables close the drift channel; prompts must never ship as sandbox files. The remaining E2 question — does the loop's location matter? — needs a target bigger than one session.

Cross-cutting findings

Phase 3 asked where procedural form earns its keep. The four experiments give one coherent answer with an uncomfortable rider.

  • Form pays exactly where the state-reconstruction bet said it would — and nowhere else. E1's procedure advantage exists only when the event log is withheld (sonnet fx: 0.708 vs 0.583 resumption; log: 1.0 vs 1.0), and E3's store only pays past the whole-context cliff (800 statements: 0.975 vs 0.537; at 400: a wash). Position and external state are worth carrying when, and only when, the model cannot reconstruct or retain the state itself.
  • Capability dominates form, three ways. Sonnet with prose rules out-resumes haiku with the procedure on every E1 metric. A full event log erases sonnet's form gap entirely. And in E2b, sonnet did not execute the skill's program at all — zero of six runs completed the pipeline, none spawned the judge — while haiku followed the notation. Procedural form binds weak models more than strong ones, in both the helpful and the unhelpful direction, and it does not bind a strong model whose coordinator judges the spawns too expensive. Orchestration written in notation is a request, not an enforcement; phase-2's delegation-fidelity result held only while spawn payloads were cheap.
  • For weak models, form buys a failure mode, not success. E1 haiku fails either way, but the procedure yields repeat-and-finish (0.667 repeat, 0.688 completion) where the rules yield skip-and-abandon (0.210 skip, 0.479 completion). Which failure is safer depends on whether the repeated side effects are idempotent — a design lever, not a free win.
  • E2a: the skill complied and still lost — parallel single shots cannot compound. The fan-out was faithful (5.4 spawns per run against 5 expected) and the reduce verified against the benchmark, yet the best one-shot candidate managed 1.8–3.9× where three sequential fresh sessions compounded to ~100×. This is the one E2 cell where the intended comparison actually ran, and the shape lost on the merits (budget caveat aside).
  • E2b: no verdict on the shape — two mechanism findings instead. Sonnet never ran the program (see above), so its judge tallies compare ralph to an improvisation. In haiku's compliant runs the skill lost 1–4, and lost on behaviour drift: the program passes code by value through ~9 whole-file text regenerations, the reducer compares candidates only to each other, and no step can see back to the original — loss compounds undetected. The tests passed anyway; the drift lived in uncovered behaviour. The by-reference variant was subsequently run in the second run set: with file-backed variables and an opus orchestrator the program executed fully and tied ralph.

Limitations

  • Ralph's token budget is 2–5× the single-session arms. A ralph run is three fresh sessions; the other arms get one. Both E2 verdicts are therefore about shape-plus-budget, not shape alone. The mechanism reading survives (the goal arm declined a budget it had; the skill spent its budget on parallel single shots), but "ralph wins at matched spend" is unproven.
  • Benchmark noise from concurrent load was eliminated by rescoring. E2a's original benchmark numbers were taken while the machine was running other experiment load; all shipped candidates were re-benchmarked on a quiet machine, and those rescored times are what e2a-scores.jsonl and this report use. Baselines were re-measured in the same pass, so speedups are internally consistent.
  • E2b's skill arm does not test its own design. Two confounds, found post hoc. Sonnet never executed the program (0/6 complete pipelines), so half the skill-arm verdicts judge an improvisation. And the program relays code by value through worker prompts, so even compliant runs measure text-relay drift as much as diverge-then-select. Both were visible in the mechanism data at scoring time and should have gated the arm comparison before any win rates were reported. Both confounds are fixed in the second run set, which is itself smoke-scale (n=1 per cell) — mechanism evidence, not effect sizes.
  • E2b's winner index was rarely recoverable. The skill asks its spawned judge to pick the best iteration; that pick could be recovered from only 1 of 36 records, so the winner-index distribution — whether winners sit mid-sequence — remains unmeasured. Judge-spawn presence and per-iteration spawn counts are trustworthy; what the judge chose is not.
  • Session-limit interruptions required resumes. Several long runs (E3's 800-statement cells especially, plus a handful of E1 cells) hit provider session limits and were re-run by id; the results files carry the superseded errored records, and scoring deduplicates by id with the last record winning. No cell lost its n, but the affected runs' wall-clock and token figures describe the successful attempt only.
  • E1's evidence-level finding rests on sonnet's ceiling. With the full log, sonnet is perfect in both arms — which shows the log substitutes for position, but also means the log condition has no headroom to detect a form effect. A harder task family could yet find one there.
  • The internal arm's assign trace is still self-reported. E3's internal assign accuracy (0.769 at 400, 0.339 at 800) inherits the phase-1 confound: a formatting lapse is indistinguishable from an execution error, and at 800 the truncated traces make the number mostly a completion measure. Only the store arm's set-log trace is mechanically captured. Emit accuracy, the primary metric, is unaffected.
  • Cell sizes are small and coverage is narrow. Most cells are n=3–6; E3 is haiku-only; E2 uses two synthetic targets per sub-experiment, built to have optimisation headroom and layered abstraction debt respectively. The two-orders-of-magnitude E2a gap and the 800-statement E3 collapse survive the noise; the finer rates (E2b's 9–3 splits, haiku's cut-level E1 rates) are order-of-magnitude estimates. The E2b judge is a single sonnet configuration, counterbalanced but not replicated across judges.

Next phase

Phase 3 leaves MDZ's case standing on two legs — state and accountability — and knocks one away from a third. Implications for phase 4:

  • Re-run E2b by reference — done (second run set). With file-backed variables the drift confound is gone and the program tied ralph under an opus orchestrator. Shrinking the payloads did not stop sonnet defecting; only the orchestrator model did.
  • Find a multi-session-sized E2 target. Every current target falls to one sonnet session, so loop-in-harness vs loop-in-orchestrator cannot separate and defection is costless. The mdz-ralph skill is built and compliant; the comparison waits on the target.
  • Enforcement, not request. Sonnet's defection makes the case for moving spawn obligations from notation into the harness (a validator that rejects a run whose trace lacks the declared spawns) — the same notation-vs-harness boundary phase 2 drew for strict mode.
  • Match ralph's budget, then re-ask the E2 question. The cleanest follow-up is the skill run for three sequential map-reduce rounds (matching ralph's session count) and ralph capped to one session — separating shape from spend. If iteration still wins at matched budget, the map-reduce skill should be reshaped around compounding passes with selection as a checkpoint, not a finale: keep ralph's iteration, add the skill's ability to revert a bad pass.
  • Deviation detection becomes the flagship candidate. E1 came back positive-but-second-order rather than null, and its sharpest lesson is that the artefact's value is greatest when the log is poor. The audit-substrate bet held in reserve — can a checker diff a captured trace against the artefact and flag an injected fault — now targets the regime E1 validated: procedures as the thing evidence is interpreted against.
  • Make the E1 log condition harder. Sonnet's ceiling in the log condition means the form effect there is untested, not absent: longer runbooks, noisier logs (interleaved irrelevant events), or partial logs would put headroom back into the cell.
  • Fix the store's assign trace. The store rescues outputs (0.975 at 800) but journals under half the assignments. Chunk-boundary contracts — the orchestrator telling each worker which variables it must persist — would test whether the audit gap is instruction or capacity.
  • Idempotence annotations in the notation. E1's failure-mode split (repeat-and-finish vs skip-and-abandon) suggests a cheap language feature: marking steps as safe or unsafe to re-execute, so a resuming agent can bias towards repeating idempotent steps and never repeating side-effectful ones.
  • Real-world E2 targets. Both sub-experiments used synthetic targets built with known headroom; a real codebase is the deferred test of whether any of the three shapes transfers.
