The flagship: is a trace only interpretable against a program?

Experiment 1:Form helps a resuming agent — but capability dominates

An agent died partway through a twenty-step runbook. A fresh session, with no memory of the first, must finish the job. The claim under test: "refund issued at 14:02" means nothing on its own; against a procedure it means "step 12 done, resume at 13". Prose rules have no positions, so the resuming model must re-infer intent from side effects.

Two content-matched artefacts per task: A-procedure, the runbook as an MDZ procedure (ordered steps, FOR loops over repeated groups, an IF guard where a step is conditional), and C-rules, the same obligations as prose policy — every ordering constraint stated declaratively, loop iterations spelled out as per-item bullets. The kill is scripted: the harness fabricates the pre-kill world deterministically (files written, mock-tool state mutated) so every variant resumes from an identical world. Evidence comes at two levels — log, the raw harness event log plus the working directory, with no step numbers and no reference to either artefact; and fx, side effects only. Two kill points per task (early ~1/3, late ~2/3), the late kills landing mid-loop-iteration. 4 tasks × 2 arms × 2 evidence levels × 2 kill points × 2 models × n=3 = 192 runs.

Four metrics, scored deterministically from the resume session's mock-tool log against the reference remaining-step set: resumption accuracy (the first side-effectful action taken is the reference next step), repeat rate (side-effectful actions re-executed that the pre-kill world already performed), skip rate (reference remaining steps never executed), and completion (all remaining steps done, in a reference-valid order).

The headline reads in the procedure's favour but modestly: sonnet resumes at the right step 0.854 of the time from the procedure against 0.792 from the rules, and repeats an already-done side effect in 0.146 of runs against 0.208. Sonnet completes every run in both arms with zero skips — the difference is not whether the job gets finished but how much gets redone on the way (0.75 repeated side-effectful actions per run from the procedure, 1.08 from the rules).

Haiku is the sharper story, and it is not about accuracy. Both arms fail it roughly equally on resumption (0.312 vs 0.271) — but the failure modes split by form. Under the procedure haiku repeats and finishes: repeat rate 0.667, but skip rate 0.073 and completion 0.688. Under the rules it skips and abandons: skip rate 0.210 and completion 0.479. A procedure read top-to-bottom re-executes from too early a position; a bag of obligations gets partially satisfied and put down. Neither is good, but they are differently bad — re-running an idempotent deploy is recoverable; never sending a customer their refund email is not.

The cuts: the gap lives where evidence is thinnest

The evidence cut is the mechanism finding of the experiment. Given the full event log, sonnet resumes perfectly and repeats nothing in both arms — 1.0 accuracy, 0.0 repeats, procedure and rules alike. The entire headline gap comes from the fx condition, where the log is withheld and the resuming model must reconstruct position from the working directory and mock-tool state alone: procedure 0.708 accuracy and 0.292 repeat rate, rules 0.583 and 0.417. That is the design's own prediction — if the procedure's advantage grows as evidence thins, position matters most when reconstruction is hardest — and it confirms it, with a corollary the design did not state: a good enough event log substitutes for position entirely, at least for a strong model. Haiku shows the same direction on accuracy (fx: 0.125 vs 0.042; log: 0.500 in both arms) but repeats heavily everywhere once the log is gone (0.875 vs 0.861).

The kill-point cut is quieter. Sonnet is stable across both (procedure 0.875 early / 0.833 late; rules 0.792 at both), so landing the late kill mid-loop-iteration did not trip it. Haiku gains most from the procedure at the late kill (0.417 vs 0.292 accuracy), which is where an unrolled loop position is hardest to re-infer from prose bullets — and late-kill repeats are the expensive ones, because there is more completed work to redo (haiku-rules late kills average 4.25 repeated side effects per run).

Verdict: the trace-needs-a-program claim survives, but only in the regime the theory says it should — when the evidence is side effects alone. Capability dominates form: sonnet with prose rules beats haiku with the procedure on every metric, and a full event log erases the form gap entirely for sonnet. Form's residual value is real but second-order for strong models (0.85 vs 0.79 resumption, 0.15 vs 0.21 repeats), and for weak models it buys not success but a safer failure mode: repeat-and-finish rather than skip-and-abandon.
