Research question 5, third attempt: does external state extend the ceiling?

Experiment 3:At 800 statements the store finally rescues

Phase 2's E3b showed the store carries real signal across a context boundary but could not show rescue: at 100 and 200 statements the whole-context baseline does not degrade, so the store only had overhead to demonstrate. E3 re-runs the same three arms — internal (whole-context execution), chunked-store (one spawned worker per ~25-statement chunk, the state tool the only cross-chunk channel), chunked-nostore (the same chunked workers guessing cross-chunk values) — at 400 and 800 statements, where phase 1 puts the internal baseline past its cliff. Generator, chunking, and scoring carry over from phase-2 e3b unchanged: emit accuracy is the LCS of emitted values against the reference trace's emits, and assign accuracy is scored from the mechanically captured set log (store arm) or the self-reported trace (internal arm). 2 sizes × 2 seeds × 3 arms × n=3, all haiku = 36 runs.

0.537internal emit accuracy at 800 (0.999 at 400)
0.975chunked-store emit accuracy at 800
0.441chunked-nostore at 800 — the store is doing the work
2/6internal 800-statement runs that completed their trace

The cliff arrived on schedule, and this time the store was standing past it. At 400 statements all three arms still hold up (internal 0.999, store 0.995, no-store 0.924) — the phase-2 picture, overhead and nothing to rescue. At 800 the internal arm collapses to 0.537 mean emit accuracy, and the collapse is truncation, not confusion: all six runs produced fenced traces, but only two ran them to completion (both scoring 1.0); the other four stopped a fraction of the way through, covering 16–48% of the reference emits. Whole-context execution at this size does not go wrong so much as give out.

The chunked store barely notices: 0.975 at 800 against 0.995 at 400, with every run producing essentially full-length output (mean emit coverage 1.00). Store-arm workers made an average of 131 set and 164 get calls per 800-statement run — the state tool is load-bearing at scale. The no-store control separates the mechanisms: chunking alone scores 0.441 at 800, and its runs split cleanly — three runs (seed 2) spawned their 21 workers and scored 0.87–0.89 by guessing well, while the other three (seed 1) spawned no workers at all and emitted nothing. Chunking without a state channel is fragile twice over: the values it cannot know, and an orchestration that can stall outright. Those three empty runs are data, not dropouts.

Two costs temper the rescue. The assign trace remains mediocre — 0.371 at 400 and 0.430 at 800 from the store's set log (the internal arm's self-reported 0.769/0.339 is not comparable; see limitations) — so the store rescues outputs convincingly and auditability only partially. And the store arm pays in wall clock: ~1,000 s per 800-statement run against ~320 s internal, with the round-trips to show for it.

Verdict: rescue confirmed, reversing phase 2's overhead-only reading. When whole-context execution genuinely breaks (internal 0.537 at 800 statements, 2/6 runs completing), load-bearing external state extends the ceiling (chunked-store 0.975) and the no-store control (0.441) shows it is the store, not the chunking, doing the rescuing. The answer to Q5 is yes — at 4× the wall clock, and with an assign trace that still captures under half the assignments.
